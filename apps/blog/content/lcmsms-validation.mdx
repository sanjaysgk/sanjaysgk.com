Computational prediction gives you candidates. Mass spectrometry tells you which ones are real. This chapter covers how LC-MS/MS (Liquid Chromatography-Tandem Mass Spectrometry) validates neoantigen predictions and why it's the gold standard for immunopeptidomics.

## Why Validation Matters

In the previous chapters, we generated 1.2 million candidate mutant peptides and predicted their MHC binding affinities. But these are **predictions**, not observations. A peptide with a predicted IC50 of 10 nM for HLA-A*02:01 might never actually be processed, loaded onto MHC, and presented on the cell surface.

The gap between prediction and reality is significant:
- Only ~1-5% of predicted strong binders are actually detected by mass spectrometry
- Many peptides are degraded before reaching the MHC loading complex
- Post-translational modifications can alter peptide identity
- Expression level of the source protein matters enormously

## How LC-MS/MS Works for Immunopeptidomics

The immunopeptidomics workflow:

1. **Cell culture** — Grow cancer cell lines (e.g., from CCLE) in sufficient quantity
2. **MHC immunoprecipitation** — Use antibodies (typically W6/32 for pan-Class I) to pull down MHC-peptide complexes from cell lysates
3. **Peptide elution** — Acid-elute the bound peptides, separating them from MHC molecules
4. **LC-MS/MS analysis** — Separate peptides by liquid chromatography, then fragment them in the mass spectrometer to determine their sequences
5. **Database search** — Match the observed spectra against a protein database to identify peptide sequences

### The Search Database Problem

This is where CAN-IMMUNE becomes critical. Standard proteomics searches use reference protein databases (UniProt). But neoantigens **don't exist in reference databases** — they're novel sequences created by somatic mutations.

To detect neoantigens by mass spectrometry, you need a **custom search database** that includes mutant peptide sequences alongside the normal proteome. This is exactly what CAN-IMMUNE provides: cancer-type-specific libraries of mutant peptides that can be appended to standard search databases.

```
Standard search database:
  UniProt human proteome (20,000 proteins)
  + Common contaminants

Neoantigen-aware search database:
  UniProt human proteome (20,000 proteins)
  + Common contaminants
  + CAN-IMMUNE mutant peptides for melanoma (N peptides)
```

### MSFragger for Fast Searching

We use MSFragger for database searching because of its speed. MSFragger uses a fragment-ion indexing approach that's 10-100x faster than traditional search engines like Mascot or SEQUEST.

For a typical immunopeptidomics dataset (50,000-200,000 spectra) searched against an expanded database (reference + mutant peptides), MSFragger completes in minutes rather than hours.

## Peptide Rescoring

Raw search engine results contain false positives. We apply a rescoring pipeline:

1. **FDR control** — Apply target-decoy approach to control false discovery rate at 1%
2. **Length filtering** — Keep only peptides of 8-14 amino acids (MHC Class I) or 12-20 (Class II)
3. **PTM filtering** — Remove peptides with excessive modifications that might indicate chemical artifacts
4. **Spectral quality** — Filter by Percolator or PeptideProphet scores

## What We've Found

The validation results from CAN-IMMUNE are encouraging:

- **Mutant peptide detection**: We've confirmed the presence of computationally predicted mutant peptides in LC-MS/MS data from multiple cell lines
- **Binding affinity correlation**: Detected mutant peptides tend to have stronger predicted binding affinities than undetected ones, validating the prediction approach
- **Cancer-type specificity**: Different cancer types show distinct mutant peptide profiles, consistent with their unique mutation landscapes

## The Feedback Loop

The real value is in the feedback loop between computation and experiment:

```
Computational prediction
    ↓
Generate candidate peptide library
    ↓
LC-MS/MS experiment with custom database
    ↓
Identify confirmed neoantigens
    ↓
Refine prediction models with new training data
    ↓
Better predictions
```

Each iteration improves prediction accuracy. This is why CAN-IMMUNE is designed as a living database — new experimental data continuously validates and refines the computational predictions.
